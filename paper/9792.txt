                                           ABSTRACT

Early nineteenth century New England banking exhibited high levels of lending to directors and
their associates (i.e., connected lending). Today many think this arrangement can lead to inefficiency
and financial fragility. This paper explores the decision making processes inside these banks and

argues that connected lending was viable when many people were involved in loan decisions. The
committees used to vote on the approval of loans are the focus. Banks that required more votes for

a given committee size prevented the approval of loans with private gains and social costs. The
historical data are consistent with the idea that higher levels of consensus in the loan committees
raised the return on assets.
