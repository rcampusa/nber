                                             ABSTRACT

We study a novel policy tool--interest rate uncertainty--that can be used to discourage
inefficient capital inflows and to adjust the composition of external accounts between short-term
securities and foreign direct investment (FDI). We identify the trade-offs faced in navigating
between external balance and price stability. The interest rate uncertainty policy discourages
short-term inflows mainly through portfolio risk and precautionary saving channels. A markup
channel generates net FDI inflows under imperfect exchange rate pass-through. We further
investigate new channels under different assumptions about the irreversibility of FDI, the
currency of export invoicing, risk aversion of outside agents, and effective lower bound in the rest
of the world. Under every scenario, uncertainty policy is inflationary.
