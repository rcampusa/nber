                                           ABSTRACT

       This paper investigates whether peer effects play an important role in retirement savings
decisions. We use individual data from the staff of a university to study whether individual decisions
to enroll in a Tax Deferred Account plan sponsored by the university (and the choice of the mutual
fund vendor for people who choose to enroll) are affected by the decisions of other employees in the
same department. To overcome the identification problems, we separate the departments into sub-
groups (along gender, status, age, and tenure lines) and we instrument the average participation of
each peer group by the salary or tenure structure in this group. Our results suggest that peer effects
are important. We find significant own-group peer effect on participation and on vendor‚Äôs choice,
but no cross-group peer effects.
